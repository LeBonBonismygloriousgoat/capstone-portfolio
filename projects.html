My octopous guessing game coded in python is exactly what the name suggests. However, there are two functions of this project: The first is to match circles to finish the game, and the second is to guess the amount of octopus species in the pacific ocean—it is important that this is a secret extension to the game that a player can access by clicking the fish shown on the screen. Once the player makes a guess, they may not repeat it again, for the code prints that the player had already made that guess. My digital pet that is also coded in python is an alien. It has two stats: Hunger, and filth. In order to make the alien happy, the playrr must get both stats to zero via the two buttons on the screen, "feed", and "clean". Once those buttons are clicked, the hunger and filth will decrease by one, and once the hunger and filth stats reach zero, the alien will be happy. Another feature of this digital pet is that its eyes follow the players cursor. Now, on the physical side of my projects, allow me to explain my "plasma popper". I constructed this when I was 12 years old, and it was amongst some of my first projects. In order to construct this, I cut a variety of bottles and hot glued them together and adding an extra seal of electrical tape over the bottles. After this, I cut a hole at the end of the long connection of bottles. Then, I sprayed the following flammable liquids to coat the inside walls of the bottles: Isopropyl alcohol, butane, lighter fluid, and ethanol. After that, I connected vinyl tubing to the opening by hot gluing it and securing it with electrical tape. I then coated the inside of vinyl tube with the aforementioned flammable materials, then attaching a lighter torch to the end of the vinyl tubing with electrical tape. Then at the other side of the bottles, I added a thin nozzle to compress the output. When igniting the lighter torch, a fire moves along the vinyl tubing, then gets compressed into plasma before being released, creating a blaster-type effect. Lastly, my drawing "robot". To construct this project, I first took a thick styrofoam prism. Then, I punctured each corner with colored pencils, making sure that 70% of the length of the colored pencils went through. I then secured the colored pencils with hotglue before attaching a small DC motor to the side of the styrofoam prism. After that, I attached a lopsided propeller to the motor. This ensures that the structure vibrates and jitters, creating motion and causing the project to form scribbles.
